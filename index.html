THE CARBONLY FIELD NOTES

Useful climate knowledge,
without the lecture.

Every number in this app is an estimate, but not a guess. These short reads show what the evidence says—and where it has limits.

THE SURPRISING BIT

Your plate usually matters more than its postcode.

For the global food system, transport accounts for about 5% of emissions. What you eat is generally more important than how far it travelled.

Read the evidence ↗
01 · FOOD

Beef and peas can be worlds apart.

Global average life-cycle data puts beef from beef herds at 99.48 kg CO₂e per kg, and peas at 0.98 kg. The difference is mostly production and land use—not food miles.

See the dataset ↗
02 · TRAVEL

Distance matters. So does how you move.

For UK travel, the 2026 reporting factors list 0.16591 kg CO₂e per km for an average petrol/diesel-unknown car, versus 0.03092 for national rail per passenger-km.

See the 2026 factors ↗
03 · POWER

Electricity has a postcode too.

Electricity emissions depend on the grid that makes it. The app’s electricity estimate uses the UK 2026 grid, transmission, and upstream factors; it is not a universal value.

How factors work ↗
04 · HONESTY

A single “carbon score” can hide a lot.

Farming impacts vary widely: the same food can differ by producer, location, and practices. Use the estimate to compare broad choices—not as a precise label for a specific brand.

Read the research ↗
05 · LANGUAGE

What does CO₂e actually mean?

Carbon dioxide equivalent combines gases with different warming effects into one number, using their warming effect over 100 years. It makes methane, nitrous oxide, and CO₂ comparable.

Understand CO₂e ↗
06 · NEXT STEP

Don’t chase perfect. Repeat the good.

Choose the lower-impact option when it is practical, then repeat it. A tracker is most useful when it helps you notice patterns, not when it makes you feel judged.

Try a comparison ↗

METHOD & SOURCES

How Carbonly calculates.

Food estimates use global life-cycle averages from Poore & Nemecek (2018), processed by Our World in Data. Travel and electricity estimates use the UK Government’s 2026 conversion-factor dataset. Values are presented with their source units and are not product-specific life-cycle assessments.
